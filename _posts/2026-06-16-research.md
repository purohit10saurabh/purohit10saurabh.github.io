## Order of proofs
In empirical research, we want to find the best experiment to do. It can be to test the weakest proof, or to explore the least explored direction. To improve a complex system, it makes sense to test the least tested component or improve the least optimized component of a complex system. It also makes a lot of sense to find what the final metric is currently most sensitive to. That maybe the most important direction. If it is not sensitive to a component, then why spend time improving it?
The idea
> Example:
<br>
We were debating whether to improve the positional embeddings in our transformer's layers. Should we stick with learnable absolute embeddings, or invest time (potentially a week or more) experimenting with 2D RoPE and learnable frequency parameters? The action-oriented mindset would be to just try it, but I wanted to find the most important direction and asked:
<br><br>
<strong><em>Is the model's output sensitive to layer choice subsuming the positional embeddings?</em></strong>
<br><br>
To test this, I did a radical experiment: I swapped all transformer layers for CNN layers with enough receptive field. <em>The output didn't change at all!</em> If you change 10 architectural details at once and the output remain identical, it's clear the final metric isn't bottlenecked by the layer type. Chasing incremental improvements here would have been wasted effort since the real bottleneck was elsewhere! <br><br>
This single, quick test saved weeks of blind architectural tweaking. Later, we discovered the true limitation was the loss function. Improving it to a more sophisticated adversarial loss and several rounds of finetuning were what pushed the project to production success.

To find the weakest link, we need an order of proofs from which we can test the weakest proof. An example of an order of weakest to strongest proofs that at many times worked for the problem I worked on is-
1. People's choice (on a task different that yours):
	- It is a common design choice like RoPE for positional embeddings.
	- Someone senior than you believes that it works. 
	- Its paper showed great results.
2. Theoretically viable: it makes sense to an extent. Hand wavy argument. Like transformer can do linear regression. Whether it is actually doing it is unknown. 
3. Experimentally proven to better than B.
4. Mathematically rigorous proof (or equivalent first principles).

3 and 4 are usually not in conflict. If they are, there is likely a bug in the experiment. The components with the weakest proofs should be tested first. 

Note that whether above order of proofs works or not depends on the problem domain and how deceptive (how many people failed to solve it) it is. For domains with more deterministic success, `people's choice` might be better than `theoretically viable` on average. The reason is that in a hard problem, when people worked on a problem without much success, they would have first tried `people's choice`. It did not work atleast in some components, hence it failed. This makes the domain deceptive and a more first principles approach might be better in such domains.

## Biases in play
It is important to be aware of unintentional biases that may be working for or against you. Some of them that I have seen in action are-

- [Law of triviality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_triviality): Researchers and engineers have a bias to give disproportionate importance to trivial details compared to the more important ones.

- A form of self-serving bias: A tendency I saw in certain good but not great engineers is that they devalue research, optimizing speed of experimentation rather than direction. Similarly, good but not great researchers can be blind to engineering, thinking that good engineering does not help research. In reality, a good experimental setup helps research by providing a conducive playground to experiment. My mental model for this is that:

<div style="background:#2a2b3d; color:#d4d4dc; border-left:4px solid #7aa2f7; padding:0.8em 1em; margin:1.5em 0; border-radius:4px;">  
$$
\text{Research speed} = (\text{Accuracy of direction}) \times (\text{Speed of experimentation})
$$
</div>

<div style="margin: 1em 0;">
<em>A pseudo-proof of this formula is:</em>
</div>

$$
\begin{align*}
\text{Research speed} &:= \frac{\text{Information gain}}{\text{Time}} \\\\
\text{Accuracy of direction} &:= \frac{\text{Information gain}}{\text{Number of experiments}} \\\\
\text{Speed of experimentation} &:= \frac{\text{Number of experiments}}{\text{Time}} \\\\
\implies
\text{Research speed} &= \frac{\text{Information gain}}{\text{Time}} = \frac{\text{Information gain}}{\text{Number of experiments}} \times \frac{\text{Number of experiments}}{\text{Time}} \\\\
&= \text{Accuracy of direction} \times \text{Speed of experimentation}
\end{align*}
$$

Research is to increase the accuracy of direction and engineering is to increase the speed of experimentation. Both have the same goal of increasing the research speed. Though the bottleneck for research speed would be the accuracy of direction if it is lower than the speed of experimentation and vice versa. 
As an example of my own bias towards direction, I've underestimated the effect of engineering aspects such as feature engineering and training hyperparameters on the overall performance of the system. 

- Anti-novelty bias: It is the tendency to avoid novel ideas. This is helpful and harmful in different situations. For example, in exploration phase, novel ideas are more important, while in exploitation phase(when you understand the system well enough), a better fit should be favored over novelty. In exploration phase, your predictions or intuitions about the problem are often wrong, hence novel ideas might be helpful. For example, in the initial phase in my research, many ideas that we expected to work were not working. So I knew that this is a deceptive problem and we needed to gain more information about the problem. Hence when I was at crossroads choosing between the seemingly equally viable long-term directions of whether to train a single model end-to-end (that we were already doing) or divide it into unsupervised and supervised training, I chose the latter. The reasons were multiple, both theoritical and empirical. Theoritically because it was a novel direction and it would narrow down whether the unsupervised or supervised model should be improved. Also, it would give us more information about the problem because we don't know what the unsupervised model learns. Empirically, in DALLE paper, they similarly tried text-to-image in a single model vs text to image embedding and image embedding to image. They said that since performance of both were similar, they chose latter because it is more interpretable. The text to image embedding model was the widely used CLIP model. ***The idea is that for a [hard problem](#easy-or-hard-problem), when multiple approaches are feasible above a threshold, the most novel approach is likely to succeed since people who couldn't solve the problem before would have missed it, which is what makes it a novel approach. Anti-novelty bias can be defined as the reason why the novel approach is more successful than the obvious one in such problems.*** It was very intriguing to me when I found that in a reranker in recommendation systems, it takes candidates above a threshold of relevance score and returns a diverse subset of the candidates, favoring diversity over relevance. In other words, it does not overoptimize for relevance, but rather optimizes it to a limit, then optimizes for diversity.

## Easy or hard problem?
When people try it and they are able to do it, it is called easy. When people tried to do it but could not do it, it is called hard or deceptive. Then there are things never tried, it is a new thing without the label of easy or hard. Given this information, there can be different approaches.

Many times we know have an idea if a problem is easy or hard. Life is simpler then. If it is easy like boiling rice, do the obvious things like follow a recipe. If it is hard, then atleast do not waste time on obvious things. What to do instead? Do something new that if done, would branch out or lead to many nice things.

The works like `Picbreeder` and `Why greatness cannot be planned` shows how discoveries specially difficult ones are made. People first do obvious things. Like make a goal or proxy/surrogate goal to solve a problem or build something. If it gets solved then, it can be called non serendipitous or easy (given the above definition "when people try it and they are able to do it, it is called easy.")

Now what happens when it does not work? That is when [serendipity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serendipity) comes into play. Inverntions like microwave and arguably alphafold were made this way. ***It is when the stepping stones for a hard problem are not known ahead of time. It is like going for a seemingly unrelated direction and when a useful thing comes in sight, going for it. During exploration phase, the key is to gain information(surprisal) rather than optimize.*** For example, to make the best F1 car today even faster, you need a lot of information about aerodynamics, internal combustion engine etc. Or asking what would happen if instead of making a hyperrealistic video generation model which is hard, we see if we can do the easier task of generate animated videos? The result might be AI Ghibli studio. Also my own work on encoding lipsync as visual latent led to a generalized variable length video encoder for diffusion models. I used the derivative of a function output w.r.t an input variable for deep neural networks finding if model output is sensitive to number of layers or the choice of layer. These were stepping stones. Though instead of trying to be novel, just remove anti-novel biases. Like people tried an idea in their task, it did not work. If there is a stronger proof than this hearsay for this idea in your task, it's worth trying.  

When your predictions working on a problem are often wrong, it means you do not have enough information about the system you are working on. Then there should be more orthogonality(explained below), exploration, information gain, derisking or reducing uncertainty. In that case, the maximum suprisal experiment is a good idea. Then when your predictions start becoming more accurate, you can start exploiting your understanding or optimizing the system. However, the ratio of exploration to exploitation should depend on the fraction of your incorrect to correct predictions in the domain.

## Orthogonality
This is an important problem solving concept.
**If the effects of going in two directions are independent, they are called orthogonal.** When it is not improving, think of a new orthogonal direction. Like in a 2D plane, if you are not improving in any direction in the plane, think of a z-axis perpendicular to the plane. Like attention is made efficient by many ML improvements like Linear Attention, Sparse Attention, etc. but if it is deteriorating results on your task, think of a direction orthogoal to ML like Systems improvement ex. Flash Attention. 

Examples-
- Improving a complex system like an industry scale search engine or a large language model. Assume that the data, model architecture not size, loss are orthogonal directions in the sense that if the effects of changing them is independent to current state. This would mean, for example, improving the data would improve the test metric irrespective of the model architecture and loss function. And if changing the architecture from A to B improves the test metric, it would do so irrespective of the data and loss. In such conditions, as a corollary, the optimal data would be same for all model archs and loss functions. Such a PCA like decomposition of the complex system into orthogonal components helps to find the most important directions to improve the system. In a well optimized systems, there can be just 1 orthogonal component to which the output is sensitive. It is because there are diminishing returns in every direction and most things would already be optimized enough.
- Solving a complex problem with 10 subproblems- Assuming 9 are solved, solve 1 problem at a time. For example, increasing model size might give better results but would cause more training time, leading to lesser sequential experiments. So find a direction orthogonal to increasing model size, like fix a small model size, iterate on it, and find its scaling laws. Even systems improvements like Flash Attention and torch.compile() are also orthogonal.
