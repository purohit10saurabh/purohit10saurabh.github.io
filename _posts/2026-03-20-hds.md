Machine learning works in high dimensional spaces. In this post, we explore some of its counterintuitive behaviours. These are sometimes come under the umbrella of the [Curse of Dimensionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curse_of_dimensionality). 
<!--more-->

## A fun puzzle!

The visualisation of this is below. Let's do a thought experiment in d-dimensional space. We start with a $d$-dimensional hypercube with sides of length 4 (stretching from -2 to 2). We divide this into $2^d$ smaller sub-cubes of side 2, one in each quadrant. Then we add a unit ball (radius 1) in each sub-cube. This is the tighest packing of $2^d$ balls in a $d$-dimensional hypercube. The centre of each of the $2^d$ balls is at $(\pm 1, \pm 1, \ldots, \pm 1)$. Now we add the largest possible ball with origin as the centre in the space between the $2^d$ balls. What is the radius of this central ball?

The math follows: The center of a corner ball sits at $(\pm 1, \pm 1, \ldots, \pm 1)$, which is exactly $\sqrt{d}$ away from the origin. Since those corner balls have a radius of 1, our central ball's radius is:

$$r_{\text{central}} = \sqrt{d} - 1$$

### 2D: Four balls in a square

Picture four unit balls in a 4x4 square. The central ball has a radius of $\sqrt{2} - 1 \approx 0.41$. It sits comfortably in the middle, minding its own business.

### 3D: Eight balls in a cube

Now it's eight unit balls packed into a 4x4x4 cube. The central ball grows a bit—its radius is $\sqrt{3} - 1 \approx 0.73$. It’s getting larger, but it's still safely tucked inside the box.

### The Surprise in Higher Dimensions

Let's turn up the dial. What happens as we keep adding dimensions? 

At $d = 9$, the central ball's radius hits exactly 2. It is now flush with the outer walls of the hypercube. 

But at $d \geq 10$, things get wild. The central ball’s radius is $> 2$. The ball that we squeezed *between* the corner balls is now bulging **outside** the original box!

## A fundamental question
Here is a fundamental question: What is the minimum number of dimensions required to have $n$ unit vectors so that each pair is nearly orthogonal? (i.e. for all $i \neq j$, $\lVert\vec{x}_i\rVert = 1$ and $\lvert\vec{x}_i \cdot \vec{x}_j\rvert \leq 0.1$) For large $n$, is it O(n) or O(sqrt(n)) or O(log(n))? The answer is O(sqrt(log(n))). This is a fun exercise to prove. It answers many questions in machine learning like:
- How many dimensions should be used in a language model? Since each vocabulary token is represented by a unit vector, any two vocabulary vectors should be far enough for the model to treat them separately.
- For using SVM for classification with large number of classes ($n$), how many dimensions should be used to separate $n$ classes by a large margin?
