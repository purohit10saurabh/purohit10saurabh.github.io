In this post, we explore some counterintuitive behaviors of high-dimensional spaces. These are often called the [Curse of Dimensionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curse_of_dimensionality). 
<!--more-->
Machine learning works in high dimensional spaces. Building the right intuitions about high-dimensional geometry helps answer many questions like:
- What is the order of dimensions needed to have $n$ almost orthogonal unit vectors? (i.e. for all $i \neq j$, $x_i \in [-1, 1]^d$ and $x_i \cdot x_j \leq 0.1$)
- How many dimensions should be used in a language model? Since each vocabulary token is represented by a unit vector, any two vocabulary vectors should be far enough for the model to treat them separately.
- For using SVM for classification with large number of classes ($n$), how many dimensions should be used to separate $n$ classes by a large margin?

The answer is surprisingly small. Building the right intuition about high-dimensional geometry reveals just how weird things get as space expands. 

## Now, for the fun part! 

Let's do a thought experiment. Imagine a $d$-dimensional hypercube with sides of length 4 (stretching from -2 to 2). We divide this into $2^d$ smaller sub-cubes of side 2, and snuggle a unit sphere (radius 1) into every single corner.

Finally, we wedge one more sphere right in the center, perfectly tangent to all the corner spheres.

The math checks out simply enough: The center of a corner sphere sits at $(\pm 1, \pm 1, \ldots, \pm 1)$, which is exactly $\sqrt{d}$ away from the origin. Since those corner spheres have a radius of 1, our central sphere's radius is:

$$r_{\text{central}} = \sqrt{d} - 1$$

### 2D: Four circles in a square

Picture four unit circles in a 4x4 square. The central circle has a radius of $\sqrt{2} - 1 \approx 0.41$. It sits comfortably in the middle, minding its own business.

### 3D: Eight spheres in a cube

Now it's eight unit spheres packed into a 4x4x4 cube. The central sphere grows a bit—its radius is $\sqrt{3} - 1 \approx 0.73$. It’s getting larger, but it's still safely tucked inside the box.

### The Surprise in Higher Dimensions

Let's turn up the dial. What happens as we keep adding dimensions? 

At $d = 9$, the central sphere's radius hits exactly 2. It is now flush with the outer walls of the hypercube. 

But at $d \geq 10$, things get wild. The central sphere’s radius is $> 2$. The sphere that we squeezed *between* the corner spheres is now bulging **outside** the original box! 

### *How can a sphere, trapped inside a box and squeezed between other spheres, grow larger than the box itself?*
I will leave it to the reader to figure out why this happens. You can unfold the below hint to get a clue.
